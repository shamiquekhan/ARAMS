Executive Summary

Microplastics — plastic particles smaller than 5mm — have been detected in tap water, bottled water, and groundwater sources worldwide. Emerging research links chronic exposure to inflammatory responses, hormonal disruption, and potential carcinogenic effects, though the dose-response relationship in humans remains an active area of study. Practical mitigation is possible through filtration, behavioral changes, and policy awareness.

Key Finding 1 — Contamination is universal
Studies across 14 countries found microplastics in 83% of tap water samples tested. Average concentrations range from 0.7 to 4.3 particles per litre in treated municipal water, rising significantly in areas with older pipe infrastructure.

Key Finding 2 — Health effects are emerging but serious
A 2024 study published in the New England Journal of Medicine found patients with microplastics in their arterial walls had a 4.5x higher risk of cardiovascular events over a 3-year follow-up period. Separately, animal studies demonstrate endocrine disruption at concentrations comparable to human exposure levels — effects mediated primarily through BPA and phthalate leaching.

Key Finding 3 — Bottled water is not safer
Bottled water contains on average 2x more microplastic particles than tap water, largely due to plastic packaging degradation. The WHO and IARC both classify this as an area of concern requiring further investigation.

What you can do
Reverse osmosis filtration removes up to 99.9% of microplastics and is currently the most effective consumer-level solution. Activated carbon block filters remove approximately 70%. Reducing plastic food and beverage containers, particularly for hot liquids, is the most impactful behavioral change.
